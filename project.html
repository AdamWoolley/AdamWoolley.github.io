How intrest rates effect house and rental prices in England and which income groups are worst effected by these changes

I wanted to use this project to examine how interest rates effected house prices in England, we all know there is a link between the cost of debt and house prices however I wanted to look into it further to see if there was a concrete link between monthly mortgage or rental payments and interest rates. My thinking behind this was to look into whether renters or home owners have it worse off when interest rates rise as they have been in the later end of 2022, as interest rates rise those owners on variable mortgages are faced with immediate mortagage payment increases that they can do little about. As rates rise the price of homes typically falls as well leaving those mortgages worse off as their equity decreases at the same time. When the Bank of England was raising rates at the end of last year it also made me curious as to how much landlords were passing on these increased costs to their tenants. As we will see from the first chart renters are often on the lowest incomes and may not be able to afford rent increases. This project aims to look at where the biggest impact lies from rate increases.

Conclusions

This chart shows that interest payments on mortgages are directly linked to the BOE base rate, I have included the buy to let rate as many private landlords will pay this rate on their loans and will adjust the rent prices accordingly. We can see in 2020 the base rate dropped due to the covid-19 pandemic and as a result the mortgage rates also dropped. If we compare this with the maps above at the end of 2020 house prices increased by the largest amount from the data we have, rent prices also increased substantially in this year. This data is evidence that as interest rates decrease house prices and rents increase, however when interest rates increase I would theorize that rents will also go up as landlords will pass on this extra cost to their customers (the renters). Bringing it back to the first chart this will squeeze the bottom earners as they have the highest rates of renting tightening already tight budgets.
